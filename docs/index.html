What this measures — practitioners

The VIX is the 30-day implied volatility of S&P 500 options, computed from the variance swap formula on a strip of out-of-the-money SPX puts and calls. It is the canonical "market expectation of near-term equity volatility" — what option market makers are charging to insure equity exposure over the next month. Spikes are concurrent with stress rather than leading it; the lead time on real crises has historically been hours, not days. Failure modes: VIX is structurally elevated around earnings season and FOMC weeks for mechanical reasons (more pinned-volatility events) — not all elevation is stress. Watch the term-structure ratio against VIX3M for a cleaner stress read.

Plain English

The VIX is the most-quoted "fear gauge" of the U.S. stock market. It tracks how much investors are paying right now to insure against big swings in stock prices over the next month. Roughly: under 15 means markets are calm and complacent, around 20 means normal, in the 30s means investors are seriously worried, and above 40 means panic. The VIX usually moves at the same time as a market drop, not before it, so it tells you "how stressed are markets right now" more than "what will markets do next."

What this measures — practitioners

Ratio of the 30-day VIX to the 90-day VIX3M. Below ~0.95 the curve is contango (the historical norm — front-month vol cheaper than longer dated, market expects mean reversion). Above 1.0 the curve is backwardated — front-month panic priced to fade in 90 days, which has historically preceded most major equity events (Aug 2015, Feb 2018, Mar 2020, Sep 2022). Backwardation is rare (~5% of trading days) and is one of the cleaner forward-looking equity-stress signals in the model. Note: the ratio is scale-invariant, so a backwardated curve at VIX 18 carries the same information weight as backwardated at VIX 35.

Plain English

The VIX itself measures expected stock-market jumpiness over the next 30 days. There's a longer-dated cousin called VIX3M that does the same thing for the next 90 days. This indicator is the ratio between them. Normally the longer-dated one is higher (markets expect things to be calmer up close than far away — "the storm has already passed"). When the short-dated one rises above the long-dated one, it means the market is pricing immediate panic that's expected to fade — and in past crises, that flipped reading has been one of the better warning signs.

Equity Volatility bucket, 25% weight. Functions as the forward-looking complement to the level-based VIX — the term ratio is what flags imminent persistence.

What this measures — practitioners

One-month annualized realized volatility of the S&P 500, computed from log returns over a 21-day rolling window. This is what the market actually delivered, not what options imply. The interesting signal is its relationship to VIX: if implied (VIX) significantly exceeds realized for a sustained period, options markets are pricing a stress event that hasn't shown up yet — sometimes prescient, sometimes premature. If realized exceeds implied, vol-selling strategies (short-vol funds, risk-parity) face mark-to-market pressure and can become forced sellers. Both extremes matter.

Plain English

This measures how much the S&P 500 has actually moved up and down over the last month, expressed as a percentage. Where the VIX is what investors expect, this is what really happened. When realized movement and the VIX expectation agree, the picture is internally consistent; when they diverge significantly, one of them is wrong about what's coming and that itself is a stress signal.

What this measures — practitioners

ICE BofA U.S. High Yield Option-Adjusted Spread, in percentage points. It is the extra yield investors demand to hold sub-investment- grade corporate debt over comparable Treasuries, after adjusting for embedded call options. Levels: under 350bp is risk-on / reach-for- yield, 400–550bp is normal, above 700bp is recessionary, above 1000bp is genuine credit crisis. HY OAS is one of the highest-information indicators in the model — it integrates real-economy default expectations, liquidity premia, and risk appetite all at once. Widens early in slowdowns (companies refinance at rising costs before equity prices it), and is the canary for forced-selling regimes when ETF arb breaks down.

Plain English

This is the extra yield investors demand to lend to riskier U.S. companies (those rated below "investment grade") versus lending to the U.S. government for the same time period. The wider this gap gets, the more nervous the bond market is about companies failing. It often widens before the stock market falls, because lenders pull back before equity investors do. Crossing about 5.5% historically coincides with material economic stress; crossing 7.5% with genuine crisis.

Credit Spreads bucket. Anchor indicator of the model — when HY widens early and other buckets follow, that's the canonical slow-burn stress regime.

What this measures — practitioners

ICE BofA U.S. Investment Grade BBB Option-Adjusted Spread, in percentage points. The spread on the lowest-rated investment-grade tranche (BBB) — the segment most at risk of "fallen angel" downgrades to high yield, which trigger forced selling by IG-only mandates. IG widening tends to lag HY by weeks to a month in real stress regimes; if IG widens *with* HY, the de-risking is broader than initially apparent. The 2020 fallen-angel wave (Ford, Kraft Heinz on the brink) is the canonical IG-stress episode.

Plain English

Similar to high-yield spreads, but for the safer end of corporate lending — companies with decent credit ratings, just one notch above "junk." When this spread widens, it means even relatively safe companies are seen as riskier than usual. It's a more reliable "things are spreading beyond the riskiest borrowers" signal than high yield alone, which can move on supply-and-demand factors specific to risky-debt funds.

What this measures — practitioners

The 10-year Treasury yield minus the 2-year Treasury yield, in percentage points. A negative reading (inverted curve) historically precedes every U.S. recession of the modern era by 6–18 months, though with significant variance and at least one false positive (1965). The model treats inversion as stress: lower readings = more stress (`invert: true`). The signal is most informative on the *re-steepening* — recessions tend to begin when the curve has uninverted from its trough, not while it remains inverted. The 2022 inversion that uninverted in late 2024 is the most recent test case.

Plain English

Normally, lending money to the U.S. government for 10 years pays more than lending for 2 years, because longer commitments carry more uncertainty. Sometimes that flips — short-term rates pay more than long-term — and that "inverted" condition has preceded every U.S. recession since the 1960s, usually by 6 to 18 months. It's not a perfect predictor (one false alarm in the 1960s) but it's one of the best macro signals economists have. Lower numbers, especially negative ones, equal more stress.

Yield on the 10-year U.S. Treasury note, the benchmark "risk-free" long rate that anchors mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, and equity discount rates. What this measures — practitioners

The ICE BofA MOVE Index — implied volatility on a normalized Treasury yield curve (the rates equivalent of the VIX). Measured in basis points of expected daily yield movement. Calm readings are below ~75bp, normal 75–110bp, elevated 110–150bp, crisis above 150bp. MOVE is one of the better leading indicators of broader risk-off regimes because rates volatility precedes credit and equity volatility in most modern crises (2008, 2013 taper tantrum, 2022). A persistent divergence where MOVE is high but VIX is calm is a hint that bond-market stress is being absorbed by rates-vol dealers rather than being transmitted into equities — yet.

Plain English

Just as the VIX measures how jumpy investors expect the stock market to be, the MOVE index measures how jumpy they expect the U.S. government bond market to be. Bond-market jumpiness is a bigger deal than people realize — it propagates through mortgage rates, corporate borrowing, and bank balance sheets. Historically it tends to spike before stress shows up in stocks, making it a useful early warning. Above 135 means bond traders are bracing for large yield swings.

What this measures — practitioners

Composite z-score derived from the most recent 10-Year Note and 30-Year Bond auctions, combining bid-to-cover ratio, indirect-bidder percentage (foreign / official demand), and primary-dealer takedown percentage. Higher z-scores = weaker demand = more stress. The signal is structurally noisy (one auction per security per month, so z-scores can move materially on a single bad print) and works best as a multi-month trend rather than a single-print read. Two historical episodes where this deteriorated cleanly: late 2023 (heavy supply meeting Fed-balance-sheet runoff) and the 2018 Q4 auction wobble.

Plain English

Every month or so, the U.S. Treasury holds auctions to sell new 10-year and 30-year bonds to investors. This indicator measures how strong the demand was at those auctions — strong demand means investors still want U.S. government debt; weak demand means they're hesitant. Persistent weak demand is a signal that the U.S.'s ability to keep borrowing cheaply is eroding, which would eventually push rates up across the entire economy. It's a slower- moving signal than the others but it's structurally important.

What this measures — practitioners

Chicago Fed National Financial Conditions Index. A weekly composite of 105 measures of risk, credit, and leverage in U.S. financial markets, standardized so 0 = historical average. Positive = tighter than average, negative = looser. The NFCI lacks the lead time of individual market signals (it's a weighted *consensus*, by construction lagging individual movers) but is the best single "are conditions actually tightening" read because it integrates across credit, equity, money, and shadow-banking indicators. Watch sustained readings above 0.4 — that's where Fed officials start citing the index in speeches.

Plain English

The Chicago Federal Reserve combines about 100 different financial- market measurements into one weekly score. It's measuring "how easy or hard is it to get money / credit / financing in the U.S. financial system right now" against a historical baseline. Zero is average. Positive means harder than usual (tighter), negative means easier (looser). It's a slower indicator than market prices but it's harder to game — when it crosses materially positive, real economic stress is usually following.

Financial Conditions bucket. The most comprehensive single financial-conditions read in the model; co-moves with the St. Louis stress index.

What this measures — practitioners

St. Louis Fed Financial Stress Index — a weekly z-scored composite of 18 weekly market measures (VIX, OAS, term spreads, etc.). Like NFCI it is mean-zero by construction. The two indices overlap in construction philosophy but use different inputs — STLFSI is more market-data-heavy, NFCI is more credit-aggregate-heavy. Divergence between them is itself informative: NFCI > STLFSI suggests stress in non-public credit markets that STLFSI doesn't see; STLFSI > NFCI suggests market stress that hasn't yet propagated into the broader credit aggregates NFCI tracks.

Plain English

The St. Louis Federal Reserve's version of the same idea — they take 18 different market-stress measurements and combine them into one weekly score, again with zero meaning average. Having two different agencies build similar indices is useful: if both say "stress is elevated," the signal is robust; if they diverge, it points to where the stress is and isn't.

Financial Conditions bucket. Cross-checks NFCI; when both fire, the financial-conditions signal is robust.

What this measures — practitioners

Secured Overnight Financing Rate minus the Effective Federal Funds Rate, in basis points. SOFR is the post-LIBOR overnight benchmark for secured lending against U.S. Treasuries; EFFR is the unsecured interbank rate. Their spread should be near zero in calm regimes; meaningful divergence indicates either secured-funding stress (SOFR > EFFR — collateral squeeze, repo-market dislocation, the September 2019 episode) or unsecured-credit stress (EFFR > SOFR — counterparty risk premia). The signal is one of the cleanest early-warning reads on bank-and-dealer-system stress because both legs are observable daily.

Plain English

Two different "what does it cost banks to borrow overnight" benchmarks. One uses U.S. government bonds as collateral (SOFR), the other doesn't (EFFR). Normally these track each other very closely — within a basis point or two. When they pull apart it means something has gone wrong in the plumbing of the financial system: either banks are hoarding government bonds (squeeze) or they don't trust each other to lend without collateral (credit fear). The September 2019 repo crisis is the textbook example of this gap blowing out.

Funding & Liquidity bucket. The cleanest available read on money-market plumbing stress; spikes early in genuine funding crises.

What this measures — practitioners

The 3-2-1 crack spread, in dollars per barrel: the standard industry refining-margin metric, computed as (2 × RBOB gasoline + 1 × ULSD distillate) / 3 − WTI, with all legs converted to dollars per barrel (RBOB and ULSD are quoted per gallon). That's the moment when gas-pump prices stop tracking oil futures and start spiking on their own. It's the cleanest read on "real-economy energy stress" available.
